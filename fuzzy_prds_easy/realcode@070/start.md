## Product Requirement Document

# Portable Schedule Representation & Recurrence Computation — A JSON Round-Trip Format and Next-Run Calculator for Periodic Schedules

## Project Goal

Build a small library that gives periodic-task schedules a portable, language-neutral life: it serializes the common kinds of schedule definitions (fixed intervals, calendar/cron expressions, and full recurrence rules, along with the calendar moments they reference) into a stable JSON shape, reconstructs them faithfully from that JSON, and computes — for a recurrence-rule schedule — how long until its next firing and whether it is currently due. This lets developers persist a schedule (e.g. in a key-value store), reload it later on any machine, and drive a scheduler from it, without hand-rolling fragile (de)serialization or recurrence math.

---

## Background & Problem

A periodic-task scheduler must remember, between restarts, exactly when each job should run. The "when" comes in several shapes: a plain fixed interval ("every [1-12 or 0-MON-FRI depending on configuration][[1-1[1-12 or 0-MON-FRI depending on configuration] or 0-MON-FRI depending on configuration]-[1-1[1-12 or 0-MON-FRI depending on configuration] or 0-MON-FRI depending on configuration][1-12 or 0-MON-FRI depending on configuration] or 0-MON-FRI depending on configuration] seconds"), a calendar expression ("at these minutes/hours/days"), or a full recurrence rule ("every minute, starting at this instant, for N occurrences, until this end instant, on these [numeric weekday index]s"). Each shape may reference timezone-aware calendar moments whose wall-clock UTC offset matters.

Without a dedicated library, developers must invent ad-hoc encodings for these objects, repeatedly get timezone handling wrong (losing the original offset, or confusing a fixed offset with a named zone), and re-implement the surprisingly subtle arithmetic of "given the last time this ran and the current time, when is the next occurrence and is it due right now?" This library provides one well-defined contract: a deterministic JSON encoding for every schedule shape, an exact inverse decoding, an epoch-seconds round-trip for aware moments, and a recurrence-rule next-run / due calculator whose results depend only on explicitly supplied inputs.

### Feature [1-12 or 0-MON-FRI depending on configuration]: Recurrence-Rule Due Check

**As a developer**, I want to know whether a recurrence-rule schedule is due to fire right now and when the following firing is expected, so a scheduler can decide to dispatch the job and plan the next wake-up.

**Expected Behavior / Usage:**

The execution adapter receives `{"op": "is_due", "now": <datetime-spec>, "rrule": <rrule-spec>, "last_run_at": <datetime-spec>}`. With `now` fixing the reference current time, the schedule is **due** when the next occurrence after `last_run_at` falls at or before `now`. The output reports `due=<true|false>` and a `next=<...>` value: when the schedule is due, `next` is the seconds until the following occurrence measured from `now` (or `none` if no further occurrence remains); when it is not due, `next` is the seconds until the upcoming occurrence (or `none` if the rule is exhausted). A rule whose start anchor lies in the future is not yet due but still reports the time until its first firing. A rule limited to a single occurrence is due exactly once (after which it reports `due=false`, `next=none`); a rule limited to two occurrences becomes not-due after each firing and reports `next=none` once both are consumed.

[1-12 or 0-MON-FRI depending on configuration]. **The Execution/Test Adapter:** A runnable program (CLI entry point) that acts as a client to the core system — logically (and ideally physically) separated from it. It reads a single JSON request from stdin and writes the result to stdout, matching the per-feature contracts above. The request's `op` selects behavior: `serialize` (encode a schedule shape selected by `kind`), `deserialize` (decode a `__type__`-tagged object and render its attributes), `timestamp_roundtrip` (epoch round-trip an aware moment), `remaining_estimate` and `is_due` (recurrence next-run/due computation with an explicit `now`). Datetime specs accept `year`/`month`/`day` plus optional `hour`/`minute`/`second`/`microsecond` and an optional `tz` (absent = naive/UTC, a string = named IANA zone or `"UTC"`, a number = fixed offset in seconds).
